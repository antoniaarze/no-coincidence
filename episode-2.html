Issue 02 · The F1 Files · Part Two

Where F1 goes from here.

New regulations, a move from ESPN to Apple TV, and the question of whether digitally-born fandom can actually last.

By Antonia 8 Min Read April 2026
Part 02 · The Future
02

Issue 02 · The F1 Files

Last issue, we broke down how Formula 1 pulled off one of the biggest audience transformations in modern sports. A dying TV product became a global cultural brand in under a decade, and they did it by rewriting the story, building an identity people wanted to belong to, and turning perception into billions in value. But now the interesting question is: can they keep it going?

Because 2026 is shaping up to be the most pivotal year for F1 in a long time. New regulations. New teams. New cars. And a brand new broadcast deal that moves the entire U.S. audience from ESPN — the platform that helped build it — to Apple TV. That's a lot of change at once. And every one of those changes carries real risk to the fandom F1 just spent eight years building.

So let's talk about the future. What's working in F1's favor, what could go wrong, and what this whole thing tells us about how cultural fandom actually works.

§ 01The Apple TV gamble

Let's start with the biggest change. Beginning in 2026, Apple TV is the exclusive home of F1 in the United States. Five-year deal, reportedly worth around $750 million — roughly $150 million a year, up from the $90 million ESPN was paying. From a revenue standpoint, it's a huge win. From a fandom standpoint? It's a genuine gamble.

ESPN didn't just broadcast F1. ESPN normalized F1. When you flipped on ESPN on a Sunday morning and F1 was right there between SportsCenter and football highlights, it sent a quiet but powerful signal: this belongs. This is a real sport. This is something people watch. And there's a really well-documented thing in psychology where the more you encounter something casually — even if you're not paying close attention — the more favorable you start to feel toward it. ESPN gave F1 that for free, millions of times over.

Apple TV is a completely different model. It's a streaming platform you have to actively pay $13 a month for. You're not going to accidentally stumble onto an F1 race while channel surfing. You have to already know you want it, and you have to want it badly enough to add another subscription to your life. That's a huge barrier compared to what existed before.

F1 has been killing it on engagement. The fans they have are obsessed. But the funnel that creates those obsessed fans starts with someone casually watching a race they didn't mean to watch.

If you cut off that casual top of the funnel, you're still left with strong engagement — but a smaller pipeline of new fans coming in over time. So the trade-off is: deeper relationships with the fans you have, fewer new fans showing up.

Apple is clearly aware of this. They're making some races and all practice sessions free. They're weaving F1 across Apple News, Apple Sports, Apple Music, even Fitness+. The play is to make F1 feel less like a paid sports subscription and more like a lifestyle that shows up everywhere in your day. Whether that actually works — whether Apple's ecosystem can replicate the casual exposure that made ESPN such a quiet powerhouse — that's the multibillion-dollar question.

§ 02Changing the product that built the brand

At the same time, the cars themselves are changing. 2026 brings the biggest regulation overhaul in over a decade. The engines are moving to a 50/50 split between electric and combustion power — nearly a 300 percent increase in electrical power. The cars will be lighter, with active aerodynamics and 100 percent sustainable fuel.

On paper, this is exactly what younger fans say they want. Sustainability. Innovation. A sport that takes the climate seriously. But here's where it gets complicated. F1's entire brand — the thing people perceive when they hear those two letters — is built on speed, sound, glamour, and adrenaline. That's what it stands for. That's what makes it feel like F1 instead of just… cars driving in a circle.

And early testing has shown the new cars are noticeably slower. Some drivers are openly criticizing them. Max Verstappen reportedly compared them to "Formula E on steroids," which is not the comparison you want when you're Formula 1.

This is one of the most interesting tensions in marketing in general: what people say they value versus what actually keeps them coming back. Younger fans absolutely care about sustainability. But they also fell in love with F1 because it felt fast, loud, and cinematic. If the racing isn't exciting, the values won't save it. People don't stay loyal to brands because the brand's ethics are correct — they stay loyal because the experience makes them feel something. And if F1 trades the experience for the values, they could win the moral argument and lose the audience anyway.

§ 03Everything working in F1's favor

Okay, that's the risk side. Now let me talk about why I actually think F1 is in a much stronger position than the doom-and-gloom takes suggest — because the world they've built around the sport is genuinely incredible.

F1 teams generated over $2 billion in sponsorship revenue in 2024. That's a record. LVMH — the luxury group that owns Louis Vuitton, Dior, Tiffany — signed an unprecedented ten-year partnership with F1. American brands have more than doubled their presence since 2018.

76% of F1 fans say sponsors actually improve their experience. In most sports, fans see sponsors as intrusions. In F1, they're seen as part of the world.

That is incredibly rare, and it means the commercial side of F1 is reinforcing the fandom instead of cannibalizing it.

And the cultural reach goes way beyond traditional sponsorship now. The Brad Pitt F1 movie — produced by Apple — became the highest-grossing sports film of all time at over $630 million. F1 partnered with Amelia Dimoldenberg for a series called Passenger Princess that generated 263 million views across platforms. Female creators have built massive communities around the sport. F1's social media following went from 18.7 million in 2018 to over 107 million by 2025, with 2.3 billion engagements that year — more than the NBA, NFL, Premier League, and Champions League combined territory.

And here's a small but really telling shift. Celebrities have always shown up at races — Monaco has been a thing forever. But the way they show up has changed. They're not coming as random famous people getting photographed. They're coming as actual fans who binge-watched Drive to Survive and now know the storylines. Ed Sheeran is a known F1 obsessive. Brad Pitt and Damson Idris both said they binged the show before making the movie. That shift — from celebrity-as-decoration to celebrity-as-fan — is a pretty good signal that F1 has crossed over from "sport people watch" to "thing people are part of."

This is the part that's really hard to undo. Once something becomes embedded in culture — once it shows up in fashion, in movies, in TikTok trends, in the way people dress for Halloween — it stops being just a thing you watch. It becomes a thing you live around. And things you live around are way harder to walk away from than things you just consume.

On top of all that, 2026 brings new teams that should help even more. Cadillac is entering as a brand new team — the first American constructor on the grid in a long time. Audi is debuting with their own engine. Ford is back in partnership with Red Bull. And this matters way more than it might sound. One of F1's persistent challenges in the U.S. has been that there's no American flag to root for. Surveys show about a third of Americans would be more interested if there were more American drivers or teams. Cadillac directly addresses that — it gives Americans an us to root for in a sport full of thems. That tribal pull is one of the deepest and most reliable forces in any kind of fandom.

§ 04A new kind of fandom

If you take a step back and compare F1 to traditional sports fandom, something interesting jumps out. Most of the great sports fanbases were built over generations — your dad took you to Yankees games, his dad took him, the love of the team got passed down like a family heirloom. The stories built up over decades. The loyalty was almost inherited.

F1's new fandom is nothing like that. It was built in five years, almost entirely through screens. People didn't inherit it — they discovered it on Netflix or social media. The shared stories aren't fifty years deep, they're five seasons of a docuseries. The way people show their fandom isn't a hat or a jersey passed down from a parent — it's an aesthetic, a vibe, an outfit.

This is a completely different kind of fandom than anything we've seen before, and we don't actually know yet how durable it is. 94 percent of current fans say they'll still be watching in five years, which is a remarkable signal. But F1 might be the first real test case of whether fandom that's born digital and grows on social media can actually last the way the old-school stuff did.

§ 05The big picture

So here's where I land. F1's fandom isn't going to shrink in any meaningful way — the infrastructure is too strong, the cultural footprint is too big, the brand is too deeply embedded now. But the shape of the fandom is going to change.

The Apple TV move will probably create a smaller but more committed U.S. core, which will affect how new fans come in. The new regulations will test whether a brand built on speed and spectacle can survive a fundamental change to the product. The continued expansion into fashion and lifestyle will push F1 even further from being "just a sport" — which is both its biggest strength and its biggest identity question. And the new American teams will either deepen tribal loyalty in the U.S. or show that flags on cars aren't enough to make Americans feel ownership.

If F1 can navigate all of that — keep the racing exciting, keep the content flowing, make Apple TV feel accessible, and hold onto the audience that found them through a Netflix show and a social media algorithm — then they don't just sustain their growth. They become the playbook everyone else copies. And honestly, a lot of leagues are already trying to.

F1's transformation isn't really about racing. It's about how our relationship with the things we love is changing.

How we discover them. How we consume them. How we build our identities around them. And how much of what we think is our own taste was actually engineered by someone with a really good marketing strategy. That's the stuff this blog is going to keep poking at.

§ 06Before you close the tab

Think about the last thing you got really into. A show, a brand, a creator. How did you actually find it? You probably didn't inherit it, and you definitely didn't rationally evaluate it. You got pulled in by a person, a story, a moment that made you feel something. That's what happened to F1 fans. And it's happening to you all the time, with almost everything you choose. You just don't usually stop to notice.

Future issues go way beyond sports — into why we'll book a worse flight just to stay loyal to one airline, why a logo on a plain t-shirt makes it worth ten times more, and why we make the choices we make without ever really questioning them.

There's always a reason.
