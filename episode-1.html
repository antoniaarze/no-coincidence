Issue 01 · The F1 Series · Part One

What changed in Formula 1.

A $4.4 billion bet, a Netflix docuseries, and the accidental invention of the playbook every sport is now copying.

By Antonia A. 8 Min Read April 2026
Part 01 · What Changed
01

Issue 01 · The F1 Series

Here's something that would have sounded absolutely insane ten years ago: Formula 1 is now one of the fastest-growing sports in the United States. Not basketball. Not football. A European motorsport where twenty drivers go in circles at 200 miles an hour. And somehow, in the span of about five or six years, it went from something most Americans had never heard of to something your friends won't stop talking about.

In 2018, the average F1 race in the U.S. pulled about 554,000 viewers on ESPN. By 2025, that number had jumped to 1.3 million, a 135% increase. The global fanbase went from about 500 million to over 825 million. Season attendance hit 6.7 million in 2025, the biggest year ever. And one in four American adults now say they follow F1 at least somewhat closely.

So… what happened? That's what this piece is about. Because the way Formula 1 grew its audience isn't luck. It's one of the most intentional, well-engineered fandom plays in modern sports history. And it starts with a $4.4 billion bet.

§ 01Rewriting the story

In January 2017, an American media company called Liberty Media bought the commercial rights to Formula 1 for $4.4 billion. And this is where the story really begins.

Before Liberty took over, F1 was run by a guy named Bernie Ecclestone. He'd been in charge for decades, and under his leadership, F1 was profitable, but it was stuck. The sport had no formal marketing department. No social media strategy. Ecclestone once said he didn't see the point in appealing to young people because they couldn't afford the luxury watches sponsors were selling. Between 2008 and 2016, F1's global TV viewership dropped by about 40%. Sponsorship revenue was declining. The sport felt exclusive in the worst way, gated off, complicated, and impossible to get into unless you already knew everything about it.

Liberty Media looked at this and realized something important: F1 didn't have a product problem. It had a story problem. Because every fandom, whether that's sports, music, movies, brands, is built on the same base. Competition. Personalities. Iconic moments. Shared stories that fans pass around and turn into legends. F1 had all of that happening on the track every single weekend. The races were dramatic. The drivers were fascinating. But none of it was reaching anyone outside the bubble. The stories existed; nobody was telling them.

So Liberty's first move was to open everything up. They relaxed social media restrictions, encouraged drivers and teams to post content, launched their own streaming service. But then they made the single most important decision in the entire transformation.

In 2019, Netflix released a docuseries called Formula 1: Drive to Survive. And everything changed.

There's actually a great story about how the show found its formula. The original idea was to focus on just one team. But the executive producer, Paul Martin, had a meeting in Abu Dhabi with Guenther Steiner, who at the time was the team principal of Haas, and within the first two minutes of talking to him, he basically realized: this guy is a character. And if people in F1 were willing to be that honest and that dramatic on camera, the show didn't need to be about racing at all. It could be about people. That single meeting changed the entire format. They crossed out the one-team idea and made the show about the personalities of the whole grid.

And that decision is the whole reason it worked. Drive to Survive didn't try to explain racing. It didn't lead with lap times or engine specifications. Instead, it gave you characters. Rivalries. Drama. Contract negotiations. Midfield politics. Team principals losing their minds in the garage. It turned drivers and bosses into personalities you cared about before you ever cared about the sport. Because you don't become a fan of something by being convinced it's technically impressive, you become a fan because you got attached to a person or a story. Drive to Survive manufactured that for millions of people who had zero prior interest in racing. You didn't need to understand DRS or tire compounds. You just needed to root for Daniel Ricciardo.

57% of Gen Z who watched a behind-the-scenes sports docuseries said it made them start watching a sport they didn't follow. About one in five current F1 fans say Drive to Survive is the reason they're a fan.

And the format worked so well that it's now basically the playbook for every other sport that wants to grow its audience. The same production company has used the Drive to Survive formula for golf (Full Swing), tennis (Break Point), cycling (Tour de France: Unchained), rugby (Six Nations: Full Contact), and track and field (Sprint). F1 didn't just transform itself. It invented the model that every league is now copying.

§ 02From watching to belonging

But here's the thing: a great story alone doesn't create lasting fandom. It gets you to watch once. What turns watching into fandom is when the thing becomes part of how you see yourself.

Think about how people identify themselves. We're all kind of made up of group memberships — where we're from, what we studied, who we voted for, the teams we root for, the brands we wear. When you say "I'm a Ferrari fan" or "I'm a McLaren fan," you're not just stating a preference. You're telling people something about who you are. You're joining a crowd with its own language, its own inside jokes, its own values. It's not really about the sport at that point. It's about belonging.

And here's what I think is the most fascinating part of the whole F1 story. The reason people become fans of teams has fundamentally changed.

Historically, if you were an F1 fan, you supported a team for one of two reasons. Either your family supported them, your dad rooted for Ferrari, so you root for Ferrari, end of story. Or you supported whoever was winning, because performance was the appeal. Both of those are pretty rational, pretty inherited. They're about tradition or about logic.

But the new generation of fans, the ones who came in through Drive to Survive and TikTok, they don't do it that way at all. They get attached to a driver first, and the team comes second. And specifically, they get attached through a character arc. The underdog who keeps getting overlooked. The comeback story after a crazy season. The championship pursuit where someone's chasing it against the odds. It's the same reason people get hooked on a TV show: you're not invested in the plot, you're invested in the people. And once you're emotionally attached to a driver's journey, you start caring about their team, their rivals, the politics, the strategy. The fandom builds outward from a person, not inward from a tradition.

People aren't inheriting their loyalties anymore. They're choosing them based on stories that move them. Which means whoever tells the best story wins.

This is what F1 unlocked, mostly through Drive to Survive and social media. Before the show, saying "I'm an F1 fan" felt niche, almost gatekept. After the show, it became aspirational. It became cool. And the data on this is amazing. 70% of Gen Z fans now say F1 represents a status or image they aspire to. 59% of newer fans say fashion and style are part of why they're interested. 77% of fans say their interest isn't even tied to a specific driver or team, they just love being part of the world.

That last statistic is the one that I find the most fascinating, because it tells you F1 isn't functioning like a sport anymore. It's functioning like a subculture. Like a lifestyle brand. People aren't showing up for the racing first; they're showing up for the community, the fashion, the vibes, the identity. The racing is just what gives the whole thing structure.

And look at who this new community is, because it completely flips the stereotype. The average F1 viewer age dropped from 44 to 32. Women now make up 42% of the global audience, up from 32T in 2018. Among Gen Z fans, the gender split is almost 50/50. The single fastest-growing demographic in all of F1 right now? 16-to-24-year-old women. In the U.S. alone, the fanbase has hit over 52 million people, and 70T of Gen Z fans engage with F1 content every day, not just on race weekends.

If you'd told someone in 2015 that the typical new F1 fan would be a 22-year-old woman in Texas who got into it through TikTok, they would have laughed at you. But that's the reality now. And it's a really good reminder that the assumptions we make about who follows what, the conventional wisdom about sports fans, brand fans, any fans, are usually way more wrong than we think.

§ 03When perception becomes value

Now here's the part that really gets me, because it ties everything back to behavioral economics and how value actually works.

If you really think about it, the racing on track didn't actually change between 2017 and 2025. Same cars, basically the same format, same circuits, same drivers winning championships. The product was largely the same. What changed was how people perceived it. F1 went from being seen as a high-end European hobby to being seen as glamorous, exciting, and culturally relevant. And that shift in perception is worth billions of dollars.

$17.1B F1's valuation in 2023 was up from $8 billion when Liberty Media bought it, a 114% increase. Revenue climbed from $2.5B to $3.2B in a single year, with operating profit up 64%.

None of that came from the sport getting better. It came from people deciding the sport meant something to them.

So here's the thing: value isn't in the product. Value is in what people perceive the product to be. A smoothie isn't worth $19 because of what's in it. A handbag isn't worth $3,000 because of the leather. F1 isn't worth $17 billion because of the engines. The actual thing matters way less than the story you've built around it and how that story makes people feel.

§ 04The wrap-up

So that's the story of what changed. A new ownership group that figured out F1 didn't need a better product, it needed a better story. A Netflix show that turned casual viewers into emotionally invested fans by making them care about people first. A digital strategy that turned the sport into a daily lifestyle. And a complete reinvention of what an F1 fan even looks like: younger, more diverse, more global, and way more obsessed than anyone could have predicted.

Growth like this doesn't just keep going automatically. What got F1 here might not be what keeps it here. And 2026 is shaping up to be the year we find out, because F1 is about to make some really big bets that could either win the next decade of growth, or undo a lot of what they just built.

That's what the next issue is about.
